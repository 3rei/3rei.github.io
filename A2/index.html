What is a keyboard?





A keyboard is a peripheral device that features a series of keys which, when pressed, send signals to a computer or other device to register input. These signals are then interpreted by the device's software to produce characters on a screen or execute specific commands.





Types of Keyboards





Keyboards are essential input devices used in various computing and typing tasks. Over the years, they have evolved into a diverse range of options, each catering to different user preferences and needs. Whether you're a casual typist, a professional gamer, or seeking ergonomic solutions, there's a keyboard type that suits your requirements.





Mechanical Keyboard

Known for their tactile feedback and distinct sound, mechanical keyboards are a favorite among enthusiasts and gamers alike. They feature individual mechanical switches beneath each key, providing a responsive and satisfying typing experience.

Membrane Keyboard

Membrane keyboards are commonly found in standard desktop setups. They use rubber dome switches to register key presses, offering a more silent typing experience compared to mechanical keyboards. While typically more affordable, they may lack the tactile response and durability of mechanical keyboards.





How they are made





1. Design and Conceptualization

The process begins with extensive research and conceptualization. Designers and engineers brainstorm ideas for the keyboard's layout, features, and aesthetics. User feedback, market trends, and ergonomic considerations play a crucial role in this phase.

Computer-aided design (CAD) software is used to create detailed 2D and 3D models of the keyboard, allowing for precise visualization and adjustments.

2. Prototype Development

Based on the finalized design, several prototypes are developed. These prototypes may be handcrafted or created using rapid prototyping techniques like 3D printing.

The prototypes are thoroughly tested by a team of experts and potential users to identify any design flaws, functionality issues, or ergonomic concerns.

3. Material Selection and Sourcing

The choice of materials is critical to the keyboard's quality and durability. Manufacturers carefully select materials for the keyboard's housing, key switches, keycaps, and internal components.

Suppliers are sourced for the required materials, and samples are evaluated to ensure they meet the necessary specifications and standards.

4. Tooling and Injection Molding

Tooling engineers create precise molds for the keyboard's plastic components using computer-aided manufacturing (CAM) software.

Injection molding machines are used to inject molten plastic into the molds, forming the keyboard's housing, keycaps, and other plastic components.

5. Key Switch Manufacturing

If the keyboard uses mechanical key switches, these switches are manufactured separately by specialized suppliers.

The process of manufacturing mechanical switches involves precision engineering, metal contacts, and various mechanical parts that provide the desired typing experience.

6. PCB Assembly

The printed circuit board (PCB) is assembled with electronic components, including key switches, diodes, resistors, and microcontrollers.

Surface-mount technology (SMT) machines place and solder the tiny components onto the PCB with high precision.

7. Keyboard Assembly

The keyboard's plastic housing is combined with the assembled PCB and key switches. The keycaps are attached to the switches.

Other features like backlights, additional buttons, and multimedia controls are integrated into the assembly, depending on the keyboard's design.

8. Testing and Quality Control

The assembled keyboards undergo rigorous testing to ensure each key functions correctly, all features work as intended, and the keyboard meets quality standards.

Quality control inspectors check for cosmetic defects, functionality issues, and adherence to design specifications.




History

Early Typing Devices




Before keyboards, typewriters were the primary writing machines, using paper and offering no easy way to undo mistakes. In 1868, the first commercially successful typewriter was introduced, marking a significant advancement in keyboard technology. The typewriter revolutionized written communication, paving the way for modern keyboards with its QWERTY layout, still used today.

Current Keyboards

Current Keyboards




Modern keyboards have evolved from typewriters, utilizing electronic technology, smoother key switches, and various connectivity options. They offer faster typing, increased customization, and seamless integration with devices. With improved comfort and portability, they have become indispensable tools for productivity and communication in the digital age.

Future Keyboards




Now, future keyboards are using a new set of switches which utilises the hall effect, with the keyboard to do it being the Wooting keyboard! The Hall Effect is a physics phenomenon where an electrical voltage is produced perpendicular to the direction of an electric current flowing through a conductive material in the presence of a magnetic field. In keyboards, Hall Effect sensors can be used to detect the proximity of magnets attached to the key switches. By adjusting the strength of the magnetic field, the actuation point of the switches can be changed dynamically, enabling faster and more precise key presses as users can customize actuation to their preference in real-time.
